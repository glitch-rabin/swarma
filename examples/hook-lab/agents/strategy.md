# Hook Strategy

## Seed Knowledge (pre-experiment baseline)

These patterns come from analysis of 10K+ high-performing social posts across LinkedIn, Twitter, and newsletters. They are starting hypotheses, not truths -- the experiment loop will validate or discard them.

### Validated Patterns

**Specificity wins**
- Hooks with specific numbers outperform vague claims by 2-3x on saves
- "47% of startups" > "most startups"
- "I lost $23,847" > "I lost a lot of money"
- The more precise the number, the more believable the claim

**Loss framing > gain framing**
- "You're losing $X/month" outperforms "You could save $X/month" on click-through
- Exception: aspirational audiences (early career) respond better to gain framing
- Loss framing works best when the loss is specific and ongoing, not hypothetical

**Story beats data for engagement, data beats story for shares**
- Story-led hooks ("Last Tuesday our entire deploy broke") get more comments
- Data-led hooks ("Deploy failures cost enterprises $4.2M annually") get more shares/saves
- Combine both: "Last Tuesday our deploy broke. It cost us $47K in 9 hours."

**Contrarian hooks need proof in the same sentence**
- "Everyone's wrong about X" without immediate evidence reads as clickbait
- "Everyone's wrong about X -- here's the data from 500 experiments" works
- The contrarian frame is the highest-variance strategy: huge wins or huge misses

**Question hooks are overused and underperform**
- Rhetorical questions ("Want to 10x your growth?") are noise
- Genuine questions with surprising answers still work: "Why do 80% of launches fail? It's not the product."
- If the reader can guess the answer, the question is dead

### Patterns to Test

- [ ] First-person confession vs third-person case study
- [ ] Single hook vs A/B hook (present both framings and let the reader pick)
- [ ] Time-anchored ("In 2024...") vs timeless hooks
- [ ] Platform-specific: what works on LinkedIn may fail on Twitter (character constraints, audience intent)

### Anti-Patterns (avoid)

- "Here's the thing about..." (stalling, not hooking)
- Emoji-heavy openers (reads as spam on professional platforms)
- Vague superlatives ("incredible", "game-changing", "revolutionary")
- Questions where you don't give the answer in the same piece
